Basics of Likelihood

In statistics, Likelihood measures how well a specific hypothesis (parameter) explains the data you've already observed.

1. Generate Observed Data

Flip a virtual coin to create a dataset. Is it a fair coin ($p=0.5$)? Or is it biased? Your data will help us find out.

2. The Likelihood Curve

The curve shows the likelihood for every possible bias (p) from 0 to 1. The higher the curve, the more likely that specific 'p' value is the true source of your data.

L(p | data) = pH × (1-p)T

For your hypothesis p = 0.5:

Likelihood = 0.000

How to read this?

While Probability is used to predict future outcomes based on a known parameter, Likelihood is used to estimate an unknown parameter based on outcomes already observed.

The Maximum Likelihood Estimate (MLE) is the value of p where the curve peaks. It is the single "best" guess for the coin's bias according to your specific dataset.
