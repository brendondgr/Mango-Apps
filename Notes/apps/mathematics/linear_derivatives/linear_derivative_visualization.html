Why is the derivative constant?

For any linear function y = mx + b, the "slope" describes how much y changes for every unit increase in x. Because a straight line never curves, this rate of change is constant across the entire line. The derivative f'(x) is simply a mathematical way of asking "what is the slope right here?". Since the slope is the same everywhere on a line, the derivative is always just the value m.
